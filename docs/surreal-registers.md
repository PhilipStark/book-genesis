# Surreal Registers

Book Genesis uses **8 adaptive surreal registers** that act as a lens on the prose. The register is not a genre — it's a filter applied to any genre. A thriller can be Lynchian. A romance can be Oneiric. The genre defines the structure; the surreal register defines the lens.

## The 8 Registers

### 1. Borgesian

**Best for:** Intellectual thriller, philosophical fiction, literary mystery

**Description:** Conceptual labyrinths, nested infinities, reality as library. The surreal is architectural and logical in its impossibility.

**Prose characteristics:** Long sentences with logically impossible embeddings. Footnotes within footnotes. Lists that contain themselves. Mirrors and maps that precede the territory.

**Reference authors:** Borges (*Fictions*), Danielewski (*House of Leaves*), Eco (*The Name of the Rose*)

**Example application:** A detective discovers that the case files she's reading contain descriptions of her reading the case files, each layer adding a detail that changes the meaning of all previous layers.

---

### 2. Lynchian

**Best for:** Psychological thriller, noir, contemporary horror

**Description:** The familiar made strange. Heavy silences, doubles, threat that comes from the mundane. The surreal is atmospheric and disturbing without explaining itself.

**Prose characteristics:** Short paragraphs with mundane details that don't quite fit. Conversations that loop. Colors and sounds that feel wrong without being technically impossible. Characters who notice the wrongness but can't articulate it.

**Reference authors:** Lynch (*Twin Peaks*), Murakami (*Kafka on the Shore*), Mariana Enriquez

**Example application:** A woman notices that her neighbor's porch light has been on for 47 consecutive days. The neighbor smiles normally. The light is normal. Everything is normal. She starts sleeping with her bedroom door locked.

---

### 3. Magical Realism

**Best for:** Family drama, generational saga, Latin American fiction, literary romance

**Description:** The extraordinary accepted as everyday. The surreal is embedded in culture, not anomaly — it's how the world works.

**Prose characteristics:** Impossible events narrated with the same matter-of-fact tone as making coffee. No character questions the impossible. The narrator doesn't signal wonder. Beauty and horror coexist without comment.

**Reference authors:** Garcia Marquez (*One Hundred Years of Solitude*), Isabel Allende, Mia Couto, Jorge Amado

**Example application:** The grandmother has been dead for three years but still makes breakfast every morning. The family sets a plate for her. The eggs she cooks taste better than anyone else's.

---

### 4. Weird Fiction

**Best for:** Cosmic horror, speculative fiction, literary sci-fi

**Description:** The incomprehensible. Entities and phenomena beyond human cognition. The surreal provokes ontological discomfort, not jump-scare fear.

**Prose characteristics:** Descriptions that circle the indescribable. Sentences that break down syntactically when approaching the weird. Colors that don't exist. Geometry that violates Euclid. Characters who understand less the more they observe.

**Reference authors:** Lovecraft (revised), Jeff VanderMeer (*Annihilation*), China Mieville

**Example application:** The biologist enters Area X and realizes the trees are growing in patterns that, if mapped from above, form sentences in a language she almost recognizes. The more trees she maps, the less she remembers why she came.

---

### 5. Oneiric

**Best for:** Romance, coming-of-age, introspective fiction

**Description:** Dream logic. Fluid transitions, dense symbolism, non-linear time. The surreal is emotional — feelings distort perception.

**Prose characteristics:** Time jumps without markers. Spaces that transform based on the character's emotional state. Repeated images that gain new meaning. The boundary between memory and present dissolves.

**Reference authors:** Clarice Lispector, Haruki Murakami, Leonora Carrington

**Example application:** She remembers the kitchen where her mother taught her to cook, except the kitchen is now underwater, and the recipe her mother is dictating is for something that hasn't been named yet, and the water is warm, and she realizes she's not remembering — she's there.

---

### 6. Kafkaesque

**Best for:** Social fiction, satire, dystopia, bureaucratic fiction

**Description:** The absurd oppression of systems. The surreal is administrative, procedural, suffocating. The protagonist accepts the impossible because there's no alternative.

**Prose characteristics:** Forms, protocols, queues, error messages. The language of bureaucracy applied to human experience. Characters who fill out forms for things that shouldn't require forms. Systems that demand compliance with rules that don't exist yet.

**Reference authors:** Kafka (*The Trial*, *The Metamorphosis*), Saramago (*Blindness*), Huxley

**Example application:** After 8 years filling out cure forms from every institution that promised to fix him — churches, clinics, hospitals — the system issued its final response: "user not found." He'd spent 8 years looking for a cure for a person who didn't exist.

*This is the register used in the proof-of-concept book, Protocolo Nao Encontrado.*

---

### 7. Carrollian

**Best for:** Fantasy, YA, advanced middle-grade, literary humor

**Description:** Inverted internal logic. Word games, paradoxes, worlds where the rules are consistently inconsistent. The surreal is playful and dangerous.

**Prose characteristics:** Dialogue that follows nonsense logic but remains internally consistent. Rules that reverse when you notice them. Characters who explain the impossible with perfect confidence. Humor that conceals menace.

**Reference authors:** Lewis Carroll (*Alice*), Neil Gaiman, Terry Pratchett

**Example application:** "The rule is simple," said the Registrar. "To enter the building, you must already be inside. To be inside, you must have entered. There's a form for paradox resolution, but you can only pick it up at the desk on Floor 3, which requires entry." She entered anyway. No one stopped her. The paradox was, apparently, only for people who asked.

---

### 8. Cronenbergian

**Best for:** Body horror, visceral sci-fi, medical thriller

**Description:** The body as territory of the surreal. Physical transformations that mirror psychological states. The surreal is organic, visceral, intimate.

**Prose characteristics:** Detailed physical descriptions that blur the line between medical precision and horror. The body doing things bodies shouldn't do, described clinically. Pain and transformation rendered with the intimacy of a lover's touch.

**Reference authors:** Cronenberg, Samanta Schweblin (*Fever Dream*), Carmen Maria Machado

**Example application:** After the third session of electroconvulsive therapy, he noticed his fingertips had become slightly transparent. Not invisible — translucent, like tracing paper. He could see the tendons moving when he made a fist. The doctor said it was a known side effect. He didn't ask known to whom.

---

## How to Choose

| If your book is about... | Consider... |
|--------------------------|-------------|
| Ideas, puzzles, reality itself | Borgesian |
| Atmosphere, dread, the uncanny | Lynchian |
| Family, culture, generations | Magical Realism |
| The unknown, cosmic scale | Weird Fiction |
| Emotions, memory, identity | Oneiric |
| Systems, bureaucracy, society | Kafkaesque |
| Rules, games, language | Carrollian |
| The body, transformation, medical | Cronenbergian |

You can also **blend registers** — a Lynchian-Kafkaesque thriller, an Oneiric-Magical Realism family saga. The orchestrator will adapt.

## The Surreal as 5th Obligation

In Book Genesis, every chapter must fulfill 5 simultaneous obligations: Plot, Character, Emotion, Theme, and **Surreal**. The surreal register must manifest in at least one moment per chapter — it can be an image, a perception distortion, an impossible element accepted as natural, or a sentence that breaks consensual logic.

The surreal is never gratuitous. If it exists "because it's beautiful" but doesn't reveal theme, character, or emotion, it's decoration and should be cut or transformed into something functional.
