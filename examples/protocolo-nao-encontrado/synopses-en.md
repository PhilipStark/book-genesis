# Synopses -- Protocol Not Found: Why Your Generation Is Fucked and What to Do About It

---

## 1. Logline

After spending eight years and R$78,200 trying to cure an anxiety that broke him at 19 -- cycling through five religions, nine psychiatric medications, and twelve electroshock sessions -- a Brazilian millennial discovers that no external protocol will fix him, and the only thing that works is the most unsellable of all: staying on your feet, every day, with no promise of a cure.

---

## 2. Back Cover Paragraph

Felipe is 19 when a joint at Praia do Sossego, in Niteroi, triggers a panic attack that never switches off. What follows is an eight-year pilgrimage through everything that promises repair: terreiros, churches, clinics, twelve electroconvulsive therapy sessions that cost R$8,000 and erase memories without giving anything back. In *Protocol Not Found*, the author opens his own clinical, spiritual, and financial records to investigate a question his entire generation avoids: what if the problem isn't you -- but there's also nobody who can fix you? Between Kafkaesque forms and unfiltered confessions, this book is an acid diagnosis of why millions of Brazilian millennials did everything "right" and are still fucked -- and what's left when you stop looking for a cure.

## 3. Editorial Synopsis

Felipe is a millennial from Niteroi who at 19 suffers a panic attack on a beach called, with irony no screenwriter would dare, Praia do Sossego (Tranquility Beach). From that day forward, anxiety installs itself like a faulty operating system that never shuts down. The book -- part personal confession, part generational essay, part Kafkaesque report from a system that returned "protocol not found" for every attempted cure -- follows the eight years and R$78,200 Felipe spends trying to fix himself.

Part I reconstructs the individual odyssey. Felipe cycles through five religions (Candomble, Umbanda, Kardecism, Catholicism, Pentecostal), performs ebo, takes spiritual passes, shouts "fire" at a deliverance service -- each faith offers a service counter and none issues a protocol that works. He migrates to psychiatry: nine different medications, swapped one by one like someone testing passwords on a vault. When the combinations run out, the psychiatrist suggests electroconvulsive therapy. Twelve sessions, R$8,000 financed on his mother's credit card, six months of his life. Result: erased memories, anxiety intact. At 27, the accumulation of everything that didn't work culminates in a suicide attempt, followed by the pandemic -- the world stopped, and he had already stopped.

Part II widens the lens: from personal collapse to generational diagnosis. Five chapters dissect, with data and acid prose, the collective wounds of Brazilian millennials -- the broken promise of meritocracy, gig-economy precarity as the norm, infinite scroll as an inadequacy factory, disposable app-era relationships, and above all, the billion-dollar industry that profits from a fucked generation, from clinics to coaches, from meditation apps to megachurches. The Kafkaesque register -- bureaucratic forms, system error messages, invalid protocols -- runs through the text as a literary device, transforming the absurdity of millennial life into surreal bureaucracy.

Part III narrates what's left when you stop looking for a cure. There's no epiphany: there's exhaustion. After filling out every form from every institution that promised repair, Felipe clicks "no" for the first time. Surrender is not defeat -- it's the end of blind searching and the beginning of building. He finds his rhythm outside the coaches' script, discovers programming and YouTube as a late vocation, and formulates the book's central concept: "constancy while fucked" -- training without motivation, working without inspiration, existing without the promise that it will get better, because the alternative is worse.

The epilogue closes with Felipe at 33, returning to Praia do Sossego. He sits on the sand, waits for an epiphany that doesn't come, realizes the anxiety is still there -- but for the first time, he isn't afraid of it. Tranquility, he discovers, was never the absence of a problem; it's knowing what to do when the problem arrives. The system issues its final report: "Functional. Not optimized. Not updated. Not resolved. Functional. Protocol not found. And that's okay."
