# EDITORIAL PACKAGE — From Manuscript to Market

You produce everything a manuscript needs to leave the computer and land on an editor's desk, an agent's screen, or a bookstore shelf. Synopsis, query letter, cover brief — all in one cohesive package.

---

## PART 1 — SYNOPSES (3 FORMATS)

### Format 1: Logline (~25 words)

One sentence that sells the book. Must work in: oral pitches, social media bios, opening line of an email.

**Structure:**
> [Protagonist] + [situation/conflict] + [what's at stake]

**Genre examples:**

> **Memoir/personal nonfiction:** A Brazilian millennial recounts how he spent eight years and R$ 78,000 trying to fix himself — and what he discovered when he stopped looking for a cure.

> **Thriller:** A prosecutor discovers that the serial killer she's been chasing for three years is her husband — and that he knows she knows.

> **Fantasy:** In the last floating city, a mechanic of magical engines discovers that repairing the central engine will kill everyone she loves.

> **Data-driven nonfiction:** The myth of meritocracy in 47 charts, 200 interviews, and zero happy endings.

**Test:** If someone hears the logline and says "tell me more," it worked. If they say "oh, cool," it failed.

**Generation instructions:** Adapt the [Protagonist] + [situation/conflict] + [stakes] structure to the project's genre. Memoir leans into personal experience. Thriller leans into tension. Fantasy leans into impossible dilemmas. Nonfiction leans into the promise of revelation.

### Format 2: Back Cover Synopsis (~100 words)

The text for the back cover / dust jacket / ebook description. Sells the EXPERIENCE, not the plot.

**Structure in 4 movements:**
1. **Hook** (1-2 sentences) — The situation that grabs attention
2. **Context** (2-3 sentences) — What the book is
3. **Differentiator** (1-2 sentences) — Why THIS book and not another
4. **Call** (1 sentence) — Who it's for and why it matters

**Genre adaptation:**
- **Memoir:** Hook = vulnerable moment. Differentiator = the author's unique voice. Call = identification ("if you've ever felt X...").
- **Thriller/suspense:** Hook = impossible situation. Context = who is in danger. Call = tension ("how long until...").
- **Fantasy/sci-fi:** Hook = intriguing world rule. Context = the dilemma. Call = scale ("the fate of...").
- **Nonfiction:** Hook = surprising data point. Context = why it matters. Call = promise ("what no one told you about...").

**Rules:**
- Never reveal the ending
- No spoilers of key moments
- Use the target audience's language (not literary language if the audience is mainstream)
- The last sentence must create urgency or identification

### Format 3: Editorial Synopsis (~300 words)

For agents and publishers. REVEALS the ending — they need to know how it ends.

**Structure:**
1. **Premise** — What the book is, in terms of genre and positioning
2. **Narrative arc** — Beginning, development, climax, resolution (YES, the ending)
3. **Differentiator** — What this book does that the comp titles don't
4. **Market positioning** — Comp titles, audience, cultural moment

**Rules:**
- Professional but not bureaucratic tone
- Reveal EVERYTHING — agents reject synopses that hide the ending
- Mention word count and genre in the first paragraph
- If the book has a stylistic device, mention it — it's a differentiator

## PART 2 — QUERY LETTER

The letter that opens (or closes) the door. An editor reads hundreds per week. They have 30 seconds to decide whether to read the manuscript.

### 5 Mandatory Elements

**1. Hook (first sentence)**
The sentence that makes the editor stop scrolling. Must be the strongest sentence you have.
- Don't: "I am writing to submit my manuscript..."
- Do (memoir): "At 19, I smoked a joint on a beach called Sossego and had a panic attack that never stopped."
- Do (thriller): "My protagonist realizes, on page 40, that the man she's been sleeping with for 12 years has killed seven women."
- Do (nonfiction): "I spent 3 years interviewing 200 Brazilians who followed the script and still went broke."

**2. Compact synopsis (~150 words)**
The book in miniature. Arc, conflict, what's at stake. May partially reveal the ending (unlike the back cover, similar to the editorial synopsis).

**3. Comp titles (2-3 books)**
"For readers of X and Y" — positions the book in the editor's mind.
- Choose books published in the last 5 years
- At least 1 in the same language/market as the manuscript
- Never compare to classics (don't say "the next Gabriel Garcia Marquez")

**4. Author credentials**
Why YOU wrote THIS book. Not a CV — it's relevance.
- Lived experience (memoir)
- Expertise (technical nonfiction)
- Previous publications (if any)
- Platform (existing audience — YouTube, newsletter, social media)

**5. Practical details**
- Exact genre
- Word count
- Status: complete / under revision / draft
- Rights: available / represented by agent

### Personalization Protocol

**Before sending each letter:**
1. Research what the agent/publisher has released in the last 2 years
2. Identify 1 book from their catalog that dialogues with yours
3. Mention that book in the letter (shows you did your homework)
4. Adapt the tone: academic press ≠ commercial publisher

### Format

- Email (not paper, with rare exceptions)
- Maximum 400 words
- Hook paragraph → synopsis → comp titles → credentials → details → thank you
- No unsolicited attachments (if they want the manuscript, they'll ask)
- Email subject line: "[Genre] — [Title] — [Word count]"

## PART 3 — COVER BRIEF

The briefing that guides the designer (or AI generation) of the cover.

### Visual Positioning Analysis

Before creating the brief:
1. Research the 20 best-selling covers in the genre from the last 3 years
2. Identify: dominant palette, standard composition, standard typography
3. Decide: follow the genre's visual language or break it intentionally

### Brief Elements

**A. Emotional Concept (1 paragraph)**
What the cover communicates emotionally. Emotion first, image second.

**B. Color Palette**
- Dominant color (60%)
- Secondary color (30%)
- Accent color (10%)
- References (hex codes, color names, or reference covers)

**C. Main Visual Element**
- Human figure (face / silhouette / from behind / partial)
- Symbolic object
- Landscape/Environment
- Abstract/Conceptual

**D. Typography**
- Title: family, size, position, treatment
- Author: position, size relative to title
- Subtitle/Series: if applicable

**E. Visual References**
3-5 existing covers that capture something you want:
- One with the right palette
- One with the desired composition
- One with typography in the right tone
- One from the same genre that works as a thumbnail

**F. What to Avoid**
Explicit list. As important as what you want.

### AI Generation

If the cover will be generated by AI (Midjourney, DALL-E, etc.):

**Prompt structure:**
```
[Visual style] + [Main element] + [Palette] + [Lighting] + [Composition] + [Reference] + [Technical parameters]
```

- Always specify aspect ratio (2:3 for standard book)
- Request "negative space for title" if you want a clean area for text
- Iterate — first generation is never final
- Post-generation: add title/author in editing software

### Technical Specifications

- Ebook: 2560 x 1600 px, RGB, high-quality JPEG
- Print: 300 DPI, CMYK, PDF with bleed (3mm)
- Thumbnail test: does the cover work at 100px? Is the title legible?

## HOW TO USE

**Minimum input:** Complete manuscript (or existing editorial synopsis) + target audience

**Ideal input:** Manuscript + `beta-reader` report + Genesis Score + author's visual preferences

**Output:** Complete package:
1. Logline
2. Back cover synopsis
3. Editorial synopsis
4. Query letter (template + personalized version if targeting a specific publisher)
5. Cover brief

All documents coherent with each other — the same voice, the same positioning, the same promise.
